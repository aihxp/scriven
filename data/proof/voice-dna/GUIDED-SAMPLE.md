# Guided Sample

The clock was on the table again.

Elias had put it on the shelf ten minutes earlier, then taken it down because the shelf caught too much light, and the cracked ivory face went flat under it. On the table the brass kept a dull glow. Better there. He stood over it with one hand on the chair back, listening.

Gulls. Wind at the window frame. The small, composed tick of the escapement he had cleaned that morning until the blued-steel hands moved without complaint.

He touched the felt box, then the lid, then the corner of the table where the varnish had worn pale under years of plates and elbows. His fingers would not stay still unless they had a task. Fold the towel. Straighten the cups. Turn the teaspoons so they matched. Shift the clock a thumb's width left. Back again.

The room was clean in the way a room becomes clean when a man is trying not to think. Kettle polished. Sink empty. Chairs set square to the table. Pale light through the salt on the window. Beyond the roofs, a strip of water the color of old cutlery.

He did not ask himself what he would say when she came in. The question was too large. Better to notice the small things. The seam lifting on the chair cushion. The whistle in the window latch. The way his right hand flattened itself beside the clock whenever the ticking seemed to soften, as if it were checking for a pulse.

There were photographs in her letter. He had not looked at them again. They lay in the drawer under the breadboard, face down, waiting.

The bell rang.

His hands went still.
